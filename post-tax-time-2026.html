Tax time 2026: what individuals & small businesses should prepare

By Arun Kalluparampil Updated for FY2026 8 min read

Tax time comes around every year, and yet for many Australians it still arrives as a last-minute scramble. The good news: a little preparation makes the whole process faster, cheaper and far less stressful. Here’s how to get ready for the 2026 financial year.

Know the key dates

The Australian financial year runs from 1 July to 30 June. If you lodge your own individual return, the usual deadline is 31 October. If you lodge through a registered tax agent like us, you may be eligible for a later lodgement date — one of the quiet benefits of using an agent.

  • Keep an eye on your income statement being marked “Tax ready” in myGov before you lodge.
  • Businesses should note their BAS and PAYG instalment dates separately.

Gather your records early

The single biggest time-saver is having your paperwork in one place before you start. For most individuals that means:

  • Income: salary and wages, bank interest, dividends, rental income, government payments and any side income.
  • Deductions: work-related expenses, donations, income-protection premiums, and costs of managing your tax affairs.
  • Records: receipts, logbooks and statements that substantiate every claim.

Don’t leave deductions on the table

You can generally claim a deduction for expenses directly related to earning your income, provided you paid for them yourself, weren’t reimbursed, and have a record. Common ones include working-from-home running costs, tools and equipment, professional subscriptions, and self-education connected to your current work.

The golden rule from the ATO: you can only claim what you’ve actually spent and can prove. When in doubt, keep the receipt — and ask.

Small business: a few extra steps

If you run a business, tax time is also a chance to take stock. Reconcile your accounts, review outstanding invoices, confirm your superannuation obligations are met, and check whether any instant asset write-off or other concessions apply to you.

A simple pre-lodgement checklist

  • All income accounted for and reconciled.
  • Deduction records gathered and totalled.
  • Bank and loan statements for the full year on hand.
  • Logbooks (vehicle, home office) up to date.
  • Any major purchases or sales of assets noted for capital gains.

When to bring in a professional

If your affairs are simple, you may be comfortable lodging yourself. But if you have a business, investment property, shares, multiple income sources, or you’re just not sure you’re claiming everything you’re entitled to, a CPA can often save you more than the fee — and the fee itself is generally deductible.
